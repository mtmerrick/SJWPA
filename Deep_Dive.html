A 'Deep Dive' Into San Joaquin Water Quality




More than 1 million people in the greater San Joaquin region have been exposed to unsafe drinking water for many years. Contaminants range from the simple and expected, like high salinity levels and low levels of dissolved oxygen, to more concerning contents such as fecal matter. Our waters are consistently contaminated with pathogens like E. Coli, coliform, enterococci, and many others, with naturally occurring chemicals like VOCs, nitrates, selenium, strontium, and vanadium, and with agricultural runoff that includes herbicides and pesticides


This water quality issue has caused many communities to face multiple environmental health threats. Nearly all Californians get clean, safe drinking water delivered to their homes. However, according to state data, turning on the tap is still risky for more than 1 million people in the San Joaquin Valley, where drinking water is among the most contaminated nationwide, according to government testing. Even worse, most residents don’t even know it, as testing of private wells is not required and most water agencies fail to provide customers with required water quality reports, or provide them in a language they can’t read.


Most affected are small, rural communities, which are disproportionately poor and Latino. Long-term fixes are in the works but many solutions will take years to implement – not enough funding is available for the people who need help immediately. The majority of residents in the San Joaquin Valley rely on groundwater for some or all of their drinking water, and the primary California groundwater basin, the Central Valley Aquifer, is deeply contaminated with a mix of man made and naturally occurring contaminants. The human-caused ones include nitrates, herbicides, and pesticides, which can persist in the environment long after first introduced. Naturally occurring contaminants can be just as worrisome, including things like arsenic and uranium.


Most water suppliers can bring groundwater up to health standards by treating it or blending it with water from cleaner sources. However, many communities get 100% of their water directly from the contaminated aquifer, and can’t afford to treat it properly. According to reports by the State Water Resources Control Board, this directly threatens public health. In Tulare County, for instance, contaminated groundwater is the source of drinking water for 99 percent of the population. Much of this contamination is tied into the use of the land and the history of its development.


San Joaquin County has an extensive network of riverways, streams, creeks and canals. Contrary to what it might seem however, very few of these are even based on natural waterways at all. Instead, most of them were created in the earlier part of the 20th century, to aid in agricultural irrigation. Additionally, most of the wetlands, marshes, and other bodies of water in the San Joaquin Valley were also man-made, mostly to serve as runoff collection and overflow for the waterway system. In what was considered at the time to be a stroke of brilliance, many of these were also set as dual-purpose reserves for native animals and migratory birds. All this is not to say the region is without natural water sources however - as mentioned previously, the San Joaquin Valley sits atop the Central Valley Aquifer, one of the largest in California. Starting before it was even properly discovered, this aquifer was used as a water source for smaller towns, and to a far bigger extent, as the primary water source for all agriculture done in the San Joaquin Valley.


However, when water systems were first being created, little was actually known of the geology of the region. As it turns out, many of the places where it made the most sense to naturally or artificially divert water to had incredibly thin and porous land separating the aquifer from the surface. Some of the locations where this is incredibly true include the natural San Luis basin, and its manmade neighbor, the Kesterson reservoir, were an estimated 40% of all water at the surface trickles into the aquifer. The other problem that the eager geoengineers of the early 1900s failed to realize was that the soil quality of the San Joaquin valley is not particularly good for plant or animal life. It is rife with many naturally occurring elements and compounds that are nearly unnoticeable in small doses, but when concentrated by runoff and drainage systems that never existed before, can easily reach toxic levels. Historically, the worst offender of all these is the chemical element Selenium, which can cause severe birth defects, along with other symptoms of heavy metal poisoning. Also, keep in mind this is in addition to many herbicides, pesticides, and other pollutants found in agricultural runoff, that all drain into these artificial groundwater sources.


The so-called ‘Kestorson Crisis’ is less well known than other major geoengineering disasters like the Salton Sea, but its effects are just as haunting. The problems were painfully apparent even several decades ago, seen across the entire food chain. Populations of fish swam around deformed. Entire flocks of birds were unable to successfully reproduce for years on end. Efforts have been continuously made to try to clean up the region for years, but much of it is still cordoned off as restricted areas due to unsafe toxicity levels. However, the toxic water still continues to leak into the aquifer, and the usage of that aquifer remains “largely unregulated and unmonitored”.


While the relatively natural contaminants are a major part of the problem, agricultural runoff is just as significant a problem. The runoff has long been known to contain not only pesticides and other chemical pollutants, but also more fecal matter than most would consider acceptable. This will only further worsen due to the growing agricultural and livestock industry in the San Joaquin Valley. This directly affects the health of the environment as a whole. Many studies have shown that crop biomass decreased due to high salinity and similarly with fecal contamination. Additionally, fish studies were performed which proved that the contaminated waters were causing a decrease in fish diversity and allowing for disease to spread quickly within the underwater ecosystem. Even if only one species is directly researched or focused on, many will be affected since the environment is dependent on many pieces to function - if one part struggles, the entire environment will as well. In addition, studies found that livestock are less productive under poorer quality water because they were more likely to become diseased or experience temporary illnesses. While it may not be as significant or immediate of a worry, this will directly affect the quality of our lives as well because the livestock are used for human consumption. Whether it’s cows for milking, beef, or even fertilization; the quality of the land will become poorer with polluted waters. As many have noticed, this highlights the interconnectedness and dependency each part of the environment has on other parts. With the poor waters circulating the fields or being served to livestock, it will make continue to make its way back around, and continue to haunt us.


As we have illustrated, the lack of clean water in the San Joaquin Valley should be of utmost concern, as it has had far-reaching effects to both the local residents as well as the wildlife that inhabit the area. Thorough detail has been presented to highlight the area’s history with water contamination and pollution, along with the fallout this has caused to its inhabitants. Despite this, lesser attention has been paid to investigating the water crisis from an environmental justice lens, in order to discern whether race and socioeconomic status has played a role in the water quality local residents have received. This has begun to reverse in recent years, however, as a handful of scientific studies have examined this issue of dissecting the role of socioeconomic status on water quality one receives, specifically in the San Joaquin Valley. .


As with other issues regarding the region’s water crisis, factors are often overlapping and thus difficult to distinguish which factors are responsible to the differing degrees. This is apparent from the collection of studies that examine the impact of socioeconomic status and drinking water quality in the region. Nearly all, if not all, of these studies have agreed to the extent that lower socioeconomic status appears to a play a role in the lesser quality of drinking water that some residents of the San Joaquin Valley receive. However, in determining the exact extent and strength of such correlation, variation and disagreement arises. This degree of difference is best illustrated in comparing two reports on this same issue. The scientific study, “Social Disparities in Nitrate-Contaminated Drinking Water in California’s San Joaquin Valley,” published their findings in the Environmental Health Perspect in 2011, summarizing that “Our findings suggest that in smaller water systems, [community water systems] serving larger percentages of Latinos and renters receive drinking water with higher nitrate levels. This suggests an environmental inequity in drinking water quality.” As illustrated, the results from this study are rather straightforward in indicating a scientifically significant difference in minority community drinking water systems versus other water systems, thus suggesting environmental drinking water inequality.


However, further assessment of other sources does not necessarily share the same degree in connection. A 2014 publication in the Journal Am J Public Health titled, “The drinking water disparities framework: on the origins and persistence of inequities in exposure,” also conducted scientific studies on the correlation between socioeconomic status and drinking water access. In analyzing their findings on the link between the two, the authors write, “The framework argues, and the Toolevillecase confirms,that there is no direct causal path between race and class and disproportionate burdens; rather, race and class are imbricated in almost all the factors and actors that have historically combined, and still combine, to produce this composite burden” (610). As opposed to the previous study which argues that the lesser qualities of water in lower socioeconomic status communities is evidence of environmental inequality, this study argues that the picture is more complicated. Instead, the authors argue that race and class has no direct path between water quality and other disproportionalities, but rather that they may form the basis of a combination of factors that result in a “composite burder” for such communities. These studies and their findings, when considered as a collection, bring an important point into the San Joaquin water crisis: there are not many studies done on the impact of socioeconomic status on water quality, and those that do conflict to the degree on correlation. Therefore more focus needs to be placed on this area of water quality studies as the Valley moves forward into the future and attempts to rectify its water issues.


California has been looking for several treatment plans to the current water crisis in the San Joaquin Valley. Among many, salinity management is one approach. Specifically, soil salinity, where ground water of high salt concentration is used for irrigation. In order to make efficient decisions, it is highly important that farmers keep track of the groundwater levels and the amount of salt in their land and water. Going further, another approach to this problem is adopting a centeralized Integrated Urban Water Policy (IUWP). This is a fundamental concept of water management forcing specific regions to manage water more responsibly. However, these IUWP proposals have had little sucess so far.


As California continues on coming up with different approaches, the state has to consider the numerous irrigation systems in the area, as well the growing population. With no clean water, farmers are unable to rely on their crops to provide food for the people of San Joaquin Valley, and the rest of the country. Crops also need great amounts of fertilizers to grow in this region's poor soil, and these fertilizers contain nitrates that can easily get into the water system. Crops can only take in a certain amount of water, salt, and even nitrates. In other words, once the crop receives the maximum amount of nitrate it can hold, the excess nitrate is washed away into the drainage water and eventually gets into the nearby rivers. In order to reduce excess nitrate, an approach is being developed where farmers would be foreced to keep track of the amounts of nitrate-containing fertilizers used for their crops.


Nevertheless, there will always be many other solutions to the water crisis. However, possibly the most promising one, which has has actually seen adoption and sucess is prioritizing the use of river water over contaminated aquifer water. Currently, seven towns in the the city of Tulare are working on using river water as a source of drinking water. In these situations, river water from snowmelt is proving to be a great solution to obtaining clean drinking water. Obviously this does not solve the agricultural problem, but this is a great step in the right direction. Ensuring that the entirity of the San Jouaquin Valley has clean drinking water is of paramount importance, and we need to keep moving forwards toward that goal while still celebrating the smaller victories we have along the way.
